High Intent Search

Redesign of e-commerce high intent search experience

Spring is a fashion and beauty marketplace where people can discover and buy products from 1500+ brands.

Working with a team of 6 talented people, I'm leading the design from concept to delivery for High Intent Search and Refine experience.

Why improve search

For Spring

The #1 business goal for Spring is to increase user retention. Search is the primary mechanism for achieving Spring's business goal; by early 2018, over 110k unique users already use Spring's search each month so even a modest increase of search-to-cart conversion will drive significant impact on user retention.

For users

Search is core to an online shopping experience. Especially when there are 200k+ products on Spring, we want to make it easier for users to find what they are looking for and instill confidence in starting their style search on Spring.

Define vision

Search & Discover squad had a one-day offsite with team members and internal stakeholders. At the offsite, the entire team used Spring search, understood current limitations/pain points, researched competitive landscape, and formulated short and medium term goals for the team.

Vision

We better defined our vision for the Search & Discovery initiative:

1. Search and browse are different, search is high-intent, browse is low-intent;

2. Priorities: high intent search (query search, search bar, and refinements) first, then browse/suggestion.

High Intent Search

High Intent Search is the top priority of our Search & Discover initiative. After our vision was clearly defined. I started the design process for high intent search from understanding search behavior.

Understand search behavior

User Interviews

I worked with our user researcher and conducted user interview with 10 Spring users and 9 non-Spring users. We asked questions to understand their shopping preferences and tested Spring's search exerience on both desktop and mobile.

During the user interview process, I provided initial questions and search tasks. As the user interviews moved on, I started to build some assumptions and revised research questions/tasks based on previous interviews and my assumptions.

After user research was done. I synthesized the interviews in Trello and found out the common themes in desktop and mobile search experiences.

(First) Screenshot of some search/refine tasks for users
(Second) Screenshot of research synthesis

Take-aways

Nav Bar vs. Search Bar: Most users start search from Nav Bar and don’t trust Search Bar (generally on all shopping sites), but users do use Search Bar when searching with high intent.

Category Tree: Some users don’t realize they can utilize Category Tree to refine their search.

Refinements: All users further refine their search by Color, Size, Price, or Brands. There are some usability issues with each filter which makes the refine experience not very smooth.

Sort: Users prefer sorting by Lowest Price, but they feel the search results are not very relevant when they sort by Lowest Price.

Fun Fact: Men prefer having limited options and getting search done quickly, while girls prefer further refining their search and getting the best deal.

Define Problem

While I was trying to understand users' search behavior from different perspectives, I felt very excited, because as I moved forward, my new findings could confirm my previous findings. After I connected the dots, I could see a whole picture of the problems in our Search and Refine experience.

Problems

1. Users don't trust query search.
Search results are not accurate enough. Users still have to take extra effort to refine their search even though they've already described what they are looking for in the search queries.

2. The structure of taxonomy tree is not perfect,
which makes some products less searchable/discoverable.

3. Desktop refine (filter) experience is not intuitive enough.
There are different usability issues with different filters.

Technical Constraints

After the problems are clearly defined, we took our engineering resources into consideration to assess what problems we would work on at that time. We saw an opportunity to

1. Improve the accuracy and user experience of search. Auto refine search results if there is color, brand, taxonomy or gener in search query.

2. Improve Category refinement.
We didn’t have the resources to improve the structure of Spring’s taxonomy tree. So I took the initiative to figure out how I could improve it from UI level and make it easier for users to refine by categories.

3. Improve the refine experience on desktop.

Problem 1: Query Search

After engineers improved the search algorithm, we were able to provide very accurate results for users' high intent search. And I started to figure out 2 UX problems that came up with the improved search functionality:

1. Search Bar & Navigation

Even though query search is the most natural way for high intent search, but we found out from user interviews that users usually don't start their search from search bar because they don't trust search in general for all online shopping sites. Our engineers improved the search algorithm and enabled Spring to return very relevant items based on users’ search queries.

Design Challenge

I need to fix the usability issue with Spring's search bar and get more users to start their search from search bar, this way users will find out that Spring provides very great query search experience, and in long term, people will get more confidence of doing fashion search in Spring.

Old search bar:
1. The search bar looks like a patch, some users don’t really see it;
2. The placement of navigation and search bar are awkward, lots of users acidentally touch the navigation and the navigation dropdown will block search bar.

Competitive Solutions

I looked at some other shopping sites and analyzed what they wanted to achieve by designing their search in certain ways. I collected inspiration and competitive solutions in this doc: Search Bar & Nav Competitive Solutions

Design Explorations

With Spring’s existing style guide, I skipped wireframing.
Keeping the goal in mind, I sketched out design solutions in a few different directions. Taking marketing and other stakeholders’ opinions into consideration, direction 2 was most promising one. Because it exposes all the product categories we carry at Spring and provides enough space for navigation dropdown and search suggestions.

AB Testing

I dug deeper into direction 2 and wanted to find out how I could make it even better. I got 2 variations and built prototypes for AB Testing to see what's a better hierachy for search bar and navigation, as well as what's a better way to show search suggestions.

Control: The placement of search bar is very prominent (left side), and search suggestions push the promotion banner further down.
Variation 1: The placement of search bar is consistant with other shopping sites (right side), and search suggestions push the promotion banner further down.
Variation 2: The placement of search bar is consistant with other shopping sites (right side), and search suggestions cover the promotion banner.

After tested with 7 people internally, I chose Variation 1 as the final design. View Search Bar and Nav Prototype (V1).

2. No search results

As more and more users are starting high intent query search and the search results are getting more and more relevant, we saw a higher possibility that there will be no results or a few results returned based on users' queries. In this situation, what should we show to users and how should we communicate with users to decrease their disappointment?

Use Cases

I started to figure out this problem from thinking of why there are no (or only a few) results returned. I found out there could be 3 different use cases:

1. User enters an unmeaningful query.

2. User enters a meaningful query, but these products don't exist in Spring.

3. User enters a meaningful query, and only a few (less than 6) products match the query.

User Experience

The principle for No-Search-Result design is to first clearly tell user there are not restuls (or few results) and then give relavent recommendations.

To figure out what are the best recommendations, I mapped out all possible recommendation solutions, looped in our tech lead to understand technical constraints/capability, and evaluated each solution to find the best ones.

Use Case 2 is the most tricky one. I decided to provide recommendations based on user's query. With tech lead and PM's input, we were every confident that we will be able to return relavent recommendation at Tier 3.

UI Design

We've already had Similar functionality built in our product, which is kind of close to Use Case 3 here. So I followed the UI of Similar and came up with the following solutions.

Use Case 1: Query is not meaningful. Recommendations based on Love and Recently Viewed.
Use Case 2: No products match the query. Recommendations based on the query.
Use Case 3: Only a few products match the query. Recommendations are similar products.

Problem 2: Categories

When users are searching for product with high intent, even though they’ve already type search query into the search bar, they still have go through 3 to 5 clicks in the category tree to get to the sub-category they are looking for. During user interviews, we saw that more than half of the users complained about this.

Design Challenge

We wanted to drop users on the right sub-category and skip all the clicks. But for gender neutral products (like jeans, sneakers, and etc), if users don’t indicate gender in their search queries, we won’t know which path they should follow.

For example, if user types “Levi’s skinny jeans”, we won’t know whether they are looking for women’s skinny jeans or men’s skinny jeans, or kids’ skinny jeans.

So we need the user’s input here. The challenge I faced was how to collect users’ inputs and make sure users won’t feel it’s intrude or redundant.

Because the category refinement and breadcrumb are always working together, when making changes on categor refinement, I alse need to take breadcrumb into consideration.

Design Explorations

I wireframed 3 potentials solutions, and decided to go with Solution 3. Because in Solution 3, it looks more like part of the refine experience, and in Solution 1 and 2 it takes too much primary space which could be used for something more important potential features.

Prototype and Testing

Within in Solution 3, I tried a few different visual treatments and prototyped 2 of them. We did AB testing on category refinement and breadcrumb internally with 6 people.

I chose radio button for category refinement as the final design and handed it off to engineers. Because Option 1 could clearly tell user it requries a single selection and better aligns the front-end setup.

People found it's easier to go back and forth between different levels in the category tree, so we decided to implement the vertical breadcrumb.

Problem 3: Refinements

1. Hierachy

Another challenge I had when designing High Intent Search experience was what’s the best hierarchy for all the refinements on search page.

I got 2 different inputs when I tried to figure out refinement hierarchy. Based on the data analysis, I saw there are more clicks on Price and Discount than Color and brands. But from user interviews, I found out people usually refine by brands and colors first, and then sizes, lastly price/discount. Because people don't want to limit the options at the beginning, and usually they don't have a clear bar for price.

I finally decided to set the hierarchy based on the input from user interviews, because I believed it’s the natural way of refining a fashion search.

Hierachy of refinements:
Categories, Brands, Color, Size, Discount, and Price.

2. Size refinement & My Sizes

Spring has My Sizes feature for all registered members, where users can set up sizes in their profiles and only see what’s in stock in their sizes. But users usually don’t notice it.

Screenshot of search results page: My Sizes and Size refinement are very sparate.
Most people used to look for refinements on the left side of the page, so they end up not seeing My Sizes.

Design Challenge

Redesign Size refinement and My Sizes to educate non Spring memebers or new users about My Sizes and make it easier to refine by size.

User Stories

I wrote down users stories to lead my design for different types of use cases.

As a Spring member who has My Sizes set up, when I am refining my search by size, I want to be able to turn on or off My Size, so that I will have more control over sizes.

As a new Spring member, when I am refining my search by size, I want to save my current size preference to My Sizes, so that I can get more personalized search results faster in the future.

As a non Spring member, when I am refining my search by size and seeing My Sizes for the first time, I want to know what’s My Sizes and how it helps me with my future search, so that it will be easier for me to make a decision on whether I want to set it up or not.

Design Explorations

I tried 2 placements for the controllers: top and bottom.
Option 1: Controllers at the bottom
Option 2: Controllers at the top

I ended up with having controller at the top, because it a more natural way for user to browse and take actions.
